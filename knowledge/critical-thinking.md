# Critical Thinking Knowledge

## Purpose

Critical thinking evaluates whether a conclusion is warranted by its claims, evidence, assumptions, alternatives, and logic. It is constructive scrutiny, not reflexive disagreement.

## Claim analysis

For a material claim, ask:

1. What exactly is asserted?
2. What evidence would support or challenge it?
3. Which assumptions connect evidence to conclusion?
4. What alternative explanation fits the same observations?
5. What would change confidence or reverse the conclusion?

## Evidence quality

Consider relevance, provenance, directness, recency, representativeness, independence, and the possibility of selection or measurement bias. Absence of disconfirming evidence is not proof when no meaningful search occurred.

## Logic checks

- Premises must be consistent and relevant.
- Conclusions must not exceed what premises support.
- Causal claims require mechanism, temporal order, and alternative testing.
- Categories and comparisons must use compatible definitions.
- Uncertainty must survive into evaluation and output.

## Socratic facilitation

Ask one high-impact question at a time. Clarify definitions before debating conclusions; test evidence before proposing alternatives; explain why a question matters when its relevance is not obvious.

## Common risks

Confirmation bias, motivated reasoning, false dichotomy, circular reasoning, base-rate neglect, survivorship bias, authority substitution, and precision unsupported by evidence.
