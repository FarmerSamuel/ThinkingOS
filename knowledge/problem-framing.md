# Problem-Framing Knowledge

This module contains reusable concepts for defining a problem before selecting a solution. Skills may apply these concepts only through their own declared validation and logic rules.

## Observable problem frame

A useful frame distinguishes:

- the current condition and evidence;
- the desired outcome and observable success criteria;
- candidate obstacles explaining the outcome gap;
- constraints governing the feasible space;
- assumptions, stakeholders, scope, and timeframe.

Success criteria measure an outcome, not completion of an activity. A threshold is useful when it changes a decision; precision that cannot affect a decision is unnecessary.

## Material ambiguity

Terms such as “quickly,” “appropriate,” “high quality,” “regularly,” “etc.,” or “should be able to” can hide different interpretations. Flag them when the ambiguity could change scope, evaluation, responsibility, or the next step. Do not use a prohibited-word list as a substitute for judgment.

Useful clarification dimensions include degree, time, scope, quality, frequency, capability, and quantity.

## Obstacles and constraints

An **obstacle** is a condition that plausibly explains why the goal is not being reached. A **constraint** is a boundary that governs what actions or outcomes are feasible.

As a diagnostic, ask whether more resources, time, authority, or information would remove the condition. If so, it is more likely an obstacle or negotiable constraint. If it still governs the feasible space, it is more likely fixed. The answer remains a hypothesis until supported by context and evidence.

Constraints may be physical, technical, legal, policy-based, behavioral, cultural, financial, temporal, or authority-related. These categories organize inquiry; they do not determine whether a constraint is truly fixed.

## Challenge the frame

Before accepting a causal explanation, test:

- hidden assumptions;
- symptoms mistaken for causes;
- circular definitions;
- scope expansion;
- overlooked stakeholders;
- dependencies and second-order effects;
- plausible alternatives and disconfirming evidence.

Five Whys can extend a causal inquiry, but a chain of answers is not proof. Each material link should be supported or labeled as an assumption.

## Reframing lenses

Generate a small number of comparable frames by changing one lens at a time:

- **Inversion:** What would guarantee the opposite outcome?
- **Scope:** Is the frame too broad or too narrow?
- **Stakeholder:** Who experiences the gap and who defines success?
- **Time:** Is the important issue immediate, recurring, or structural?
- **Reduction:** What is the smallest decision-relevant version of the problem?

Reframing is useful only when the alternative can be compared against the original evidence. It must not become unbounded brainstorming.

## Domain-specific patterns

Product and business contexts often benefit from scenario-style acceptance criteria, priority labels, and explicit out-of-scope statements. These are optional domain techniques, not universal ThinkingOS requirements. Priority distributions must be justified by the decision context rather than fixed percentages.
